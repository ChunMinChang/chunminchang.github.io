How It Works

To estimate the unobserved target's location over time with a map, particle filtering(PF) plays the key part in this project. There are two main models in PF. State model will calculate the next location from previous location, movement data and its possible error. Observation model will correct the estimated location by the observed information. If the math details are ignored, from the perspective of physical significance, the basic steps are generally as the following:
1)Spread all the particles on possible locations on the given map
2)Calculate the target's movement vector $\vec{V}$ from sensor data
3)Move particle along with a vector $\vec{V^\prime}$, $\vec{V^\prime}$ is a combination of $\vec{V}$ and its possible error $\vec{e}$
4)If the particles cross the wall on the map, then they will be eliminated
5)The target's location will be concluded by the surviving particles (Repeat to (2) if receving new data)

Abstract

One of the fundamental features of the Internet of Things (IoT) is the ability to track its user's location over time as a way to provide service in the right context. While many localization and tracking techniques using cameras and RF-based techniques have been proposed, their ability is rather limited owing to the requirement of deploying tracking infrastructure; moreover, they cannot operate when outside coverage area. To overcome this limitation, we propose encounter-based cooperative tracking for proximity-enabled wireless nodes. These nodes, carried by the targets, can sense their trajectories (e.g., by inertial sensing) and each other's proximity while roaming inside a mapped area, without the need to access to localizing infrastructure such as beacons. We model their locations over time on the map as particles and apply particle filtering as the basis for tracking. Our contribution is to augment the base technique with encounter information to dramatically reduce the number of possible particles in the model by spatial-temporal intersection. Therefore, our technique is effective even when there are more than one possible matches of the node's trajectories.
